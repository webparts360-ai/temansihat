Naluri – Competitor Briefing for Corporate & Insurer Market

Naluri is a regional digital therapeutics and corporate wellness platform selling outcomes‑driven, AI‑augmented coaching programmes to employers and insurers in Southeast Asia.[web:47][web:52][web:56] From a competitor’s lens, they win where they combine measurable health outcomes, insurer‑friendly economics, and multi‑disciplinary coaching; to beat them you need sharper clinical proof, tighter local integration, and faster execution on product and sales.[web:52][web:57][web:60]

Core Position

B2B/B2B2C digital health for employers and insurers; app used by employees/policyholders as end users.[web:52][web:56][web:57]

Differentiator

Evidence‑based digital therapeutics, multi‑disciplinary coaches, and insurer‑aligned outcomes/ROI story.[web:52][web:57]

Our Edge Opportunity

Own clinic/TPA integration, operational fulfilment, SME‑friendly deployment, and faster product iterations.[web:60]

Business model and go‑to‑market

  • Core model: B2B/B2B2C digital health company serving employers and insurers; employees and policyholders use the app as the delivery channel.[web:52][web:56][web:57]
  • Revenue streams: contracted wellness and digital therapeutics programmes for corporates, health programmes embedded into group insurance plans, and subscription options for uncovered individuals.[web:47][web:51][web:52][web:57]
  • ROI positioning: Naluri markets 4x ROI in as little as 6 months based on health risk reduction and claims savings.[web:57]

Competitive insight: your sales narrative must lead with risk reduction, claims savings, and concrete ROI per employee, not just wellbeing engagement.

Solutions, features, and benefits

Solution stack

  • Digital therapeutics for obesity, diabetes, hypertension, depression, anxiety, stress and other chronic conditions.[web:51][web:57]
  • Employee wellness & EAP services including psychological support, mental health programmes, and advocacy.[web:52][web:54][web:59]
  • Insurer health programmes with risk‑stratified prevention/management tiers and outcomes‑based models.[web:57]

Key features

  • Multi‑disciplinary care team: psychologists, dietitians, fitness coaches, medical advisors, financial coaches, etc.[web:51][web:52]
  • Digital tools: food and thought journals, daily modules, planning tools, and progress dashboards.[web:51][web:52]
  • AI‑augmented triage and summarisation for coaches, plus risk screening for depression, anxiety, stress and metabolic risk.[web:51][web:52][web:57]
  • Corporate reporting on population risk levels and health improvements.[web:52][web:54][web:59]

Benefits they sell

  • Employers: lower medical costs, fewer chronic disease claims, improved productivity, measurable improvements within ~6 months.[web:49][web:52][web:57]
  • Insurers: reduced payouts, expanded insurable populations, and justification to embed Naluri in group plans.[web:52][web:57]
  • Users: convenient, holistic support across mental, physical, and financial wellbeing.[web:51][web:52][web:54]

Market momentum and traction

  • Founded around 2017 by Azran Osman‑Rani and Jeremy Ting, bringing strong operator and health‑systems credibility.[web:50][web:51][web:58]
  • Backed by regional investors including Integra Partners, Pathology Asia, M Venture Partners, and Sumitomo Corporation Equity Asia.[web:53][web:58]
  • Has raised at least ~US$7M pre‑Series B and additional US$5M for regional expansion.[web:53][web:58]
  • Serves 75+ employers across multiple sectors in Malaysia, Singapore, Thailand, and Indonesia.[web:58]
  • Embedded as an EAP/digital health partner for organisations like Malaysia Aviation Group.[web:55][web:59]

Naluri SWOT – Competitor View

Strengths
  • Clinical and outcomes credibility with digital therapeutics positioning and reported 4x ROI, 60% clinically significant outcomes and up to 50% risk reduction.[web:52][web:57]
  • Strong integration with insurers and large employers, embedding into group insurance plans.[web:57][web:58]
  • Holistic, multi‑disciplinary coaching across mental, physical, behavioural, and financial domains.[web:51][web:52]
  • Experienced leadership and reputable investors that reassure enterprise buyers.[web:50][web:53][web:58]
  • Regional ASEAN footprint that appeals to MNCs needing multi‑country coverage.[web:55][web:58]
Weaknesses
  • Complex, “heavy” programmes that may be slower and harder to deploy for SMEs.[web:52][web:57][web:60]
  • Engagement friction: sustained journaling and coach interactions can be demanding for some users.[web:51][web:52]
  • Less focus on deep outpatient operational fulfilment (panel billing, ambulances, physical networks).[web:52][web:56]
  • Perceived as a premium solution compared with lighter EAP/wellness tools.[web:57][web:60]
Opportunities (for us)
  • Own local clinic/TPA integration, cashless outpatient flows, and HR benefits reconciliation in Malaysia.[web:60]
  • Win verticals with sector‑specific journeys (manufacturing, logistics, plantations, etc.).
  • Combine AI triage with hard fulfilment (clinics, meds delivery, ambulances, on‑site screenings).[web:57][web:60]
  • Offer simpler, SME‑ready packages with faster deployments.
Threats (to us)
  • Lock‑in through insurer products and large multi‑year corporate contracts.[web:57][web:58]
  • Brand as the default digital health partner for mental health and chronic disease in ASEAN.[web:52][web:55]
  • Data and outcomes flywheel that strengthens their AI and sales story over time.[web:52][web:57]

How we win: do more, better, faster

1. Sharpen the value proposition beyond “wellness” ›

Own harder business metrics like outpatient cost per employee, sick days, and time‑to‑return‑to‑work, not just engagement or stress scores.

Design sector‑specific programmes so that generic regional offerings look misaligned next to us.

2. Out‑localise them in Malaysia ›

Integrate deeply with panel clinics, TPAs, HRD Corp, and MOH priorities so we become the obvious choice for SMEs and GLCs.[web:60]

Invest heavily in Bahasa Malaysia, Manglish and multilingual content plus culturally tuned coaches.

3. Beat them on UX and product velocity ›

Make onboarding extremely light, rely more on passive data (wearables, HRIS) and micro‑interactions than on heavy journaling.

Use AI directly in the user experience (action plans, summaries, nudges), not just in coach tooling.

4. Compete on commercial model and speed ›

Offer 8–12 week low‑friction pilots with very clear outcome metrics and minimal IT work.

Use flexible pricing (PEPM, outcomes‑linked bonuses, tiered packages) to unlock SMEs and mid‑market buyers.[web:60]

5. Build a differentiated moat ›

Combine digital triage and coaching with physical fulfilment: clinics, on‑site screenings, medicine delivery, and ambulance tie‑ins.[web:52][web:56]

Integrate with payroll, attendance, and safety data to create unique insights linking health to productivity and safety.
